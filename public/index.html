FILE 01: The Post-War Partition

In 1945, World War II ended and the dust settled on a broken Germany. The Allies—the United States, Great Britain, France, and the USSR—had won a difficult victory, but their work was far from over. Important questions remained: How would the Allies deal with a defeated Germany? What sort of government would the nation have? Before a permanent government could be established, who would rule the land?

The Allies decided to divide Germany into four occupation zones. Each major power controlled a specific region: the three zones in the West were administered by the U.S., Great Britain, and France, while the zone in the East was ruled by the Soviet Union (U.S.S.R.).

Berlin—Germany’s capital—remained the single most important city. Because of its significance, the Allies also divided Berlin into U.S., French, British, and Soviet sectors. Berlin, in a sense, became a microcosm of the divided nation. This arrangement created a unique problem: a democratic, capitalist island was now completely surrounded by Communist-controlled territory.

FILE 02: The Berlin Airlift

In June 1948, the Cold War reached a breaking point. The Soviet Union, hoping to take full control of the city, blocked all roads, canals, and railways leading into West Berlin. This was known as the Berlin Blockade. Over 2 million people were suddenly cut off from food, coal, and medicine.

The Western Allies faced a choice: surrender West Berlin or go to war. Instead, they chose a third option: an Airlift. For nearly a year, American and British planes landed in Berlin every few minutes, carrying everything from flour to fuel. It was the largest humanitarian flight operation in history, proving that the West would not abandon the city.

FILE 03: Operation Little Vittles

During the Airlift, an American pilot named Gail Halvorsen noticed children watching the planes at Tempelhof Airport. He realized they had no sweets or chocolate. He promised to drop candy from his plane the next day.

To make sure the children knew which plane was his, he told them he would wiggle his wings as he approached. He used handkerchiefs as tiny parachutes to safely drop chocolate bars and gum. Soon, other pilots joined in. This act of kindness, known as "Operation Little Vittles," turned the former enemies into lifelong friends.

FILE 04: The Wall Goes Up (1961)

While the Airlift saved Berlin in 1949, the tension never left. By 1961, the East German government was desperate to stop people from fleeing to the West. In the middle of the night on August 13, 1961, they began building a barrier that would change the world.

Soldiers tore up streets and installed miles of barbed wire. This sudden border literally split the city in half, running through neighborhoods and even separating families. Within days, the wire was replaced by concrete blocks, creating a permanent seal between East and West.

FILE 05: The "Death Strip" & Peter Fechter

As the Wall grew from a wire fence to a massive concrete barrier, the East German government added a "Death Strip"—a wide area of raked sand, floodlights, and guard towers. Anyone caught in this zone was shot on sight. Despite the danger, hundreds attempted to escape by tunneling, flying hot air balloons, or simply running.

One of the most tragic stories is that of 18-year-old Peter Fechter. In August 1962, exactly one year after the wall began, Fechter and a friend attempted to climb the wall near Checkpoint Charlie. While his friend made it over, Fechter was shot by East German guards while still on the wall. He fell back into the "Death Strip" on the Eastern side.

In a moment that shocked the world, Fechter lay wounded for nearly an hour, screaming for help in plain view of Western journalists and onlookers. The East German guards did not help him, and the Western soldiers were forbidden from crossing into the East to rescue him. His death became a symbol of the Wall's brutality and the cold reality of the "Iron Curtain."
