FILE 01: The Post-War Partition

In 1945, World War II ended and the dust settled on a broken Germany. The Allies—the United States, Great Britain, France, and the USSR—had won a difficult victory, but their work was far from over. Important questions remained: How would the Allies deal with a defeated Germany? What sort of government would the nation have? Before a permanent government could be established, who would rule the land?

The Allies decided to divide Germany into four occupation zones. Each major power controlled a specific region: the three zones in the West were administered by the U.S., Great Britain, and France, while the zone in the East was ruled by the Soviet Union (U.S.S.R.).

Berlin—Germany’s capital—remained the single most important city. Because of its significance, the Allies also divided Berlin into U.S., French, British, and Soviet sectors. Berlin, in a sense, became a microcosm of the divided nation. This arrangement created a unique problem: a democratic, capitalist island was now completely surrounded by Communist-controlled territory.

FILE 02: The Wall Goes Up (August 1961)

By 1961, East Germany (the GDR) was facing a crisis. Thousands of its citizens, many of them highly skilled workers and professionals, were fleeing to West Berlin every single day. This "brain drain" threatened to collapse the East German economy. To stop the exodus, the Soviet-backed government decided to seal the border once and for all.

In the early morning hours of August 13, 1961, East German soldiers and construction workers began tearing up streets and stringing miles of barbed wire through the heart of Berlin. This operation was conducted in total secrecy under the cover of darkness. Families who lived on opposite sides of the street suddenly found themselves separated by a military line they were no longer allowed to cross.

At first, the "Wall" wasn't a wall at all—it was a tangle of barbed wire and concrete posts backed by armed guards. However, within days, workers began replacing the wire with hollow gray bricks. This initial structure physically split the city, cutting off subway lines, phone wires, and even bricking up the windows of apartment buildings that faced West Berlin. What began as a temporary fence would eventually evolve into a massive system of concrete and death strips.
