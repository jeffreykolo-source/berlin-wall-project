FILE 01: The Post-War Partition

In 1945, World War II ended and the dust settled on a broken Germany. The Allies—the United States, Great Britain, France, and the USSR—had won a difficult victory, but their work was far from over. Important questions remained: How would the Allies deal with a defeated Germany? What sort of government would the nation have? Before a permanent government could be established, who would rule the land?

The Allies decided to divide Germany into four occupation zones. Each major power controlled a specific region: the three zones in the West were administered by the U.S., Great Britain, and France, while the zone in the East was ruled by the Soviet Union (U.S.S.R.).

Berlin—Germany’s capital—remained the single most important city. Because of its significance, the Allies also divided Berlin into U.S., French, British, and Soviet sectors. Berlin, in a sense, became a microcosm of the divided nation. This arrangement created a unique problem: a democratic, capitalist island was now completely surrounded by Communist-controlled territory.

FILE 02: The Berlin Airlift

In June 1948, the Cold War reached a breaking point. The Soviet Union, hoping to take full control of Berlin, used a blockade to try to force the West out. The USSR blocked all roads, canals, and railways leading into West Berlin (This was known as the Berlin Blockade). Over 2 million people were suddenly cut off from food, coal, and medicine. Stalin calculated that the West would not simply let those people die nor risk a war over Berlin.

The Western Allies faced a choice: surrender West Berlin or go to war. Instead, they chose a third option: an Airlift. For nearly a year, American and British planes landed in Berlin every few minutes, carrying everything from flour to fuel. It was the largest humanitarian flight operation in history, proving that the West would not abandon the city. At the height of the Berlin Airlift, planes were landing in West Berlin every 30-45 seconds. In all, the Allies made 278,228 total flights and sent in slightly over 2.3 million tons of supplies.

FILE 03: Operation Little Vittles

The Airlift was ultimately a success, but it was a stressful time: both for people inside and outside of West Berlin. During the Airlift, one an American pilot named Gail Halvorsen decided to take action to spread some good will. Noticing children had no sweets or chocolate, he promised to drop candy from his plane the next day. In the process, earned the nickname the "Candy Bomber."

To make sure the children knew which plane was his, he told them he would wiggle his wings as he approached. He used handkerchiefs as tiny parachutes to safely drop chocolate bars and gum. Soon, other pilots joined in. This act of kindness, known as "Operation Little Vittles," helped win over the people of West Berlin. For the U.S. it was in the words of some a "public relations coup." Rather than looking like imperialists, the U.S. appeared to the world to be on a humanitarian effort to help the people—especially the children—of Berlin.

FILE 05: The "Death Strip" & Peter Fechter

As the Wall grew from a wire fence to a massive concrete barrier, the East German government added a "Death Strip"—a wide area of raked sand, floodlights, and guard towers. Anyone caught in this zone was shot on sight. Despite the danger, hundreds attempted to escape (more on the escape attempts later).

One of the most tragic stories is that of 18-year-old Peter Fechter. In August 1962, exactly one year after the wall began, Fechter and a friend attempted to climb the wall near Checkpoint Charlie. While his friend made it over, Fechter was shot by East German guards while still on the wall. He fell back into the "Death Strip" on the Eastern side.

In a moment that shocked the world, Fechter lay wounded for nearly an hour, screaming for help in plain view of Western journalists and onlookers. The East German guards did not help him, and the Western soldiers were forbidden from crossing into the East to rescue him. His death became a symbol of the Wall's brutality and the cold reality of the "Iron Curtain."

FILE 06: The Expansion (1961–1980s)

The Berlin Wall was not built overnight; it evolved through four distinct generations. What started as a barbed-wire fence in 1961 became a sophisticated, multi-layered military complex. By the 1970s, the GDR (East Germany) introduced "Border Wall 75," consisting of L-shaped concrete segments that were nearly 12 feet high.

This "modern" wall featured a smooth pipe on top to make it impossible to grip. Behind the primary wall lay the Death Strip, which was eventually equipped with signal wires that triggered alarms, hidden spikes (known as "Stalin’s Grass"), and 302 watchtowers. Guards were ordered to fire on anyone attempting to cross, making the border a high-tech fortress of isolation.

FILE 07: The Escape Artists

Despite the "Death Strip," the desire for freedom led to incredible feats of engineering. People escaped by digging long tunnels under the wall, flying over it in home-made hot air balloons, and even ramming armored buses through checkpoints. One man even used a zip-line to slide from a rooftop in the East to a building in the West.

The statistics are staggering. Approximately 5,000 people successfully escaped to West Berlin. However, the cost was high. At least 140 people were confirmed killed at the Wall, though some historians believe the number is much higher. For many, the risk of death was preferable to a life without liberty.

FILE 08: The Fall of the Wall

In the late 1980s, the Soviet leader Mikhail Gorbachev began introducing reforms like Glasnost (openness). Protests erupted across Eastern Europe. On the evening of November 9, 1989, an East German official named Günter Schabowski accidentally announced at a press conference that travel restrictions were being lifted "immediately."

Thousands of East Berliners rushed to the checkpoints. The guards, confused and having no orders to fire on such a large crowd, eventually opened the gates. People from both sides climbed the wall, cheering and dancing. Within a year, Germany was officially reunified, and the Cold War’s most hated symbol was torn down by the people themselves.
